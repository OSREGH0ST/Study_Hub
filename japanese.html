Counter Kana Used For Tips & Irregularities
~tsu つ Generic objects (when unsure) Only up to 10. Uses Japanese readings: Hitotsu, Futatsu, Mittsu…
~nin 人 People 1 = Hitori, 2 = Futari (irregular). From 3: Sannin, Yonin…
~hon 本 Long, cylindrical objects (bottles, pens, umbrellas) 1,6,8,10 → "p": Ippon, Roppon. 3 → "b": Sanbon
~mai 枚 Flat, thin objects (paper, tickets, shirts) Fully regular. Ichimai, Nimai, Sanmai…
~hiki 匹 Small/medium animals (cats, dogs, fish) 1,6,8,10 → "p": Ippiki, Roppiki. 3 → "b": Sanbiki
~kai 回 Frequency (number of times) Glottal stop on 1,6,8,10: Ikkai, Rokkai, Hakkai, Jikkai
~dai 台 Machines, vehicles, furniture (cars, TVs, beds) Fully regular. Ichidai, Nidai, Sandai…
~soku 足 Pairs of footwear (shoes, socks) 1,8,10 → glottal stop: Issoku, Hassoku. 3 → "z": Sanzoku
~tō 頭 Large animals (horses, cattle, elephants) Glottal stop on 1,6,8,10: Ittō, Rokutō, Hattō, Jittō
~hai 杯 Cups, bowls, glassfuls 1,6,8,10 → "p": Ippai, Roppai. 3 → "b": Sanbai
~ko 個 Small, round objects (eggs, apples) Glottal stop on 1,6,8,10: Ikko, Rokko, Hakko, Jukko
~satsu 冊 Bound volumes (books, notebooks, magazines) Glottal stop on 1,8,10: Issatsu, Hassatsu, Jissatsu

⚖️ Grammar Limits — dake / shika / mo

だけ (dake) + Affirmative

〜だけ あります

"Only / Just" — neutral fact.
Hitotsu dake arimasu.
(There is only one.)

しか (shika) + Negative

〜しか ありません

"Only / Nothing but" — implies insufficiency.
Hitotsu shika arimasen.
(There is only one — and that's not enough.)

Counter + も (mo) + Negative

〜も ありません

"Not even one" — complete absence.
Ippon mo arimasen.
(There is not even a single one.)

Key difference: だけ is neutral ("I have only 3"). しか carries a feeling of "unfortunately only" or "not enough". も means there is literally zero.

When to use which form?

~ます (Masu Form)

Use case: Polite, everyday conversation. You use this with teachers, strangers, and coworkers.

Example: たべます (I eat / I will eat)

Dictionary Form

Use case: Casual conversation (with friends), finding words in the dictionary, and as a base for advanced grammar.

Example: たべる (I eat / I will eat)

~て (Te Form)

Use case: The "glue" form. Used to connect actions, ask for favors (~てください), or express ongoing actions (~ています).

Example: たべて (Eat and...)

~ない (Nai Form)

Use case: The casual negative. Used with friends to say you won't do something, or with ~でください to say "Please don't".

Example: たべない (I don't eat)

Making the ~て (Te) Form from ~ます

Drop the ~ます and look at the syllable right before it to know which ending to use.

Group 1: U-Verbs (The Rule-Breakers)
Before ~ます is い, ち, り (i, chi, ri)→ って (tte) *かいます → かって
Before ~ます is み, び, に (mi, bi, ni)→ んで (nde) *のみます → のんで
Before ~ます is き (ki)→ いて (ite) *かきます → かいて
Before ~ます is ぎ (gi)→ いで (ide) *およぎます → およいで
Before ~ます is し (shi)→ して (shite) *はなします → はなして

⚠️ The One Exception: いきます (To go) becomes いって (not いいて).

Group 2: Ru-Verbs (The Easy Ones)
Just drop ~ます and add てたべます → たべて (tabete)
Just drop ~ます and add てみます → みて (mite)
Group 3: Irregular Verbs
To do (します)→ して (shite)
To come (きます)→ きて (kite)

Why we need it: The ~て Power-Ups

The ~て form is "glue." You attach these helper words to it to create advanced grammar.

~ている (Ongoing Action / State)

1. Currently doing it.
2. A state resulting from a past action (e.g., being married).

たべて います

(I am eating right now)

~てある (Intentional State)

Shows a state that exists because someone intentionally did it in the past (e.g., the window has been opened).

かいて あります

(It has been written [by someone])

~てください (Please do...)

The polite way to ask someone to do something.

まって ください

(Please wait)

~てから (After doing...)

Used to say "Action A happens, and then Action B happens."

たべて から、ねます

(After I eat, I will sleep)

~てもいいです (Permission)

Used to grant permission or ask "Is it okay if I...?"

みても いいですか？

(May I look?)

~てはいけません (Prohibition)

A strong rule or prohibition. "You must not do this."

のんで は いけません

(You must not drink)

~てしまう / ~てしまいます (Oops / Finished)

Shows that you finished something completely, OR that you did something by accident and regret it.

わすれて しまいました

(I completely forgot / I accidentally forgot)

Dictionary Form (Casual Present)

This is the "original" form of the verb before you make it polite.

From ~ます to Dictionary

Group 1: Change the 'i' sound before ~ます to an 'u' sound.

のみます → のむ (nomu)

かきます → かく (kaku)

From ~ます to Dictionary

Group 2: Drop ~ます and add る (ru).

たべます → たべる (taberu)

みます → みる (miru)

Irregulars
します (To do)→ する (suru)
きます (To come)→ くる (kuru)

の Possessive Pronouns
The Rule
To say "Mine," "Yours," or "His," just add の (no) to any person. It never changes, no matter what object follows it!
Singular (One Person)
My / Mine 私の (Watashi no)
Your / Yours あなたの (Anata no)
His 彼の (Kare no)
Her / Hers 彼女の (Kanojo no)
Its それの / その (Sore no / Sono)
Plural (Multiple People)
Our / Ours 私たちの (Watashitachi no)
Your (plural) あなたたちの (Anatatachi no)
Their (General/Male) 彼らの (Karera no)
Their (Female) 彼女らの (Kanojora no)
Who's? だれの？ (Dare no?)
Quick Tip: In Japanese, if it's already obvious who you're talking about, they often leave the possessive out entirely to keep it "clean."

Modifying Nouns

I-Adjectives: Attach directly BEFORE the noun.
(あたらしい くるま)

Na-Adjectives: Add な (na) before the noun.
(しずかな へや)

Negative Form (Present)

I-Adjectives: Drop い (i) add くない (kunai).
(たかくない)

Na-Adjectives: Drop な (na) add じゃない (janai).
(げんき じゃない)
